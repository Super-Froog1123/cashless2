Barriers To Overcome

Cash-oriented Elderly Citizens

In Japan, a growing phenomenon called "Kodokushi," or "lonely death," refers to elderly individuals who die alone and remain undiscovered for days, weeks, or even months. The property left behind by the deceased elderly may be inherited, sold, or abandoned.

Terrence Poon, the managing director of Japan Starway Travel Corporation, was born in Hong Kong and has lived in Japan for 40 years.

Poon explained that when cleanup crews were hired to clear those elderly‚Äôs properties, they sometimes find stacks of cash - tucked in wardrobes, hidden under floorboards, or stashed in secret compartments- amounting of tens of millions of yen, or even hundreds of millions of yen.

Concerns Over Privacy

In 2002, Japan introduced the Basic Resident Register Network, assigning citizens unique 11-digit ID codes containing personal details, such as name, birthdate, and address. The following year, the government issued IC-chip Resident Registration cards linked to these numbers. However, both network and cards faced strong public and political opposition. Despite backlash, the Supreme Court upheld their constitutionality in 2008.

To boost Japan's digital economy, the government introduced 12-digit My Number cards with embedded IC chips in 2016. The cards serve as official identification and can be used for various services, including taxes, health insurance, and facial recognition. However, the adoption of My Number card remains slow and incomplete, about 25% of Japanese citizens still have not obtained the cards.

‚ÄúSome people are rejecting it because they think all the information would be stolen by the government,‚Äù Yoshino explained. ‚ÄúPrivacy is a very important aspect in Japan.‚Äù

When it comes to e-payment, Yoshino said that some Japanese are concerned about who can know how much they spend and where they consume when using a card.

Acceleration in Adoption

To accelerate adoption, the Japanese government and e-payment services providers have implemented a series of cashless initiatives.

In 2019, the Point Reward Program launched in Japan offered a 2% to 5% rebate for cashless purchases at participating stores and online shopping sites. The program also subsidized retailers by covering the costs of adopting payment terminals and part of the transaction fees.

The program ended in June 2020, with a 9%-12% higher number of card users at participating businesses, according to the Center for Advanced Research in Finance at University of Tokyo.

Popular mobile payment services, such as PayPay, Line Pay and Rakuten Pay, offer loyalty points and cashback rewards to consumers.

Points and reward programmes are an important consideration for consumers, especially the younger ones. A survey in March 2025 by Statista revealed that 66.2% of respondents used digital payments primarily for reward points or discounts.

Chinese international student Huang began using PayPay after discovering he could earn cashback by linking his My Number card to the app. Last year, however, he used Rakuten Pay more due to its higher rebate.
